• Letter fom Gallipoli,18 August 1915: Front
    On 18 August 1915, after nearly at fortnight at Gallipoli, Bert Mitchell wrote to his father in Onehunga:

    " … We are all very dirty. Paper is terribly scarce and we only get two pieces a week or so. The boys write on cigarette cases etc … "
    G. G. M. Mitchell. Letter, 18 August 1915. From: G. G. M. Mitchell. Papers. NZMS 531.

  • Letter fom Gallipoli, 10 September 1915: Pg1
    In September Bert was invalided out to Malta with an infected shrapnel wound, acute dysentery and fever:

    " … The conditions are so unhealthy on the Peninsula as to be absolutely adverse to the return of good health. The dead are everywhere, the ground we were occupying having been extensively fought over, and many of our men and Turks are only a couple of feet beneath the surface, and when a gusty wind arises, many of the bodies are exposed. Then too, where we constructed our first line of trenches, we were unearthing the dead and we had to remove their bodies to fresh cemeteries … "
    G. G. M. Mitchell. Letter, 18 August 1915. From: G. G. M. Mitchell. Papers. NZMS 531.

  • Care of the foot
    David Earwaker’s little notebook is full of information on the care of the foot. This was vital for soldiers who were frequently immersed in cold, wet and insanitary conditions and unable to change their socks regularly. Trench foot, a form of fungal infection, could have severe consequences and lead to amputation if not dealt with.
    David Earwaker. Notebook. [Kindly loaned by the Earwaker family of Dairy Flat.]
  • Field service card: Front
    Field service cards were not subject to censorship and ensured that those at home knew the sender was still alive. The troops disliked them. Bert Mitchell told his father they were "about as non-committal as a card could be."
    Field service card. 1915. Ephemera Collection.

  • Walter Fowler letter: page 1
    Walter Fowler of the New Zealand Field Artillery took part in the march into Germany by the Army of Occupation when the war ended. In a diary letter to his sister he describes German reaction to their arrival and his own feelings.

    " Dec. 21. Well we are in the Hun land & the sooner we are out of it the better. We crossed the border at 2.30 & instead of smiles we got frowns. Some just looked away while others cast their eyes on the ground. The boys had decorated their guns & limbers up with flags etc. We recognize that it must be humiliating for them, but I am afraid we forget all their faults too quickly, yet one cannot be cruel to them. Mind you make them pay up for all they have done. If their reception of us is as rotten as the weather it will be cold."
    Walter Fowler. Letter, 1918. From: Walter Fowler. Letters. NZMS 483.
