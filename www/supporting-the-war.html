• Souvenir programme of Grand Concert

    On the outbreak of war, patriotic societies sprang up all over New Zealand, dedicated to raising money for a wide range of causes, from the Belgian refugees to wounded soldiers. In 1915 a Federation of New Zealand War Relief Societies was established to regulate the monies raised.

    Concerts, galas, sporting events, lectures and carnivals were all used for fund-raising purposes.
    Programme. Souvenir programme of Grand Concert held in aid of the Belgian Relief Fund, 17 November 1914. Ephemera Collection.

  • To korero Aotea

    The residents of Wanganui, Wairarapa and Rangitikei raised enough money in six weeks to fully equip and staff the Aotea Convalescent Home near Zeitoun Camp in Egypt. It opened on 25 November 1915 and eventually accommodated 200 patients.

    In total patriotic societies in New Zealand raised over six million pounds.
    Contributed by the patients of the Aotea Convalescent Home, Heliopolis, Cairo. Te korero Aotea. Cairo: Whitehead, Morris, 1918.

  • Pictorial war lecture

    Those at home could follow the progress of the war through newspapers, illustrated papers like the Auckland weekly news or lectures presented by authorities such as the Rev. Monckton, Vicar of St Peter's, Takapuna. Visits to the cinema to watch newsreels of the war also increased. In 1916 13 cinemas were built in Auckland, in addition to the 17 theatres already licensed to show films.
    Pictorial war lecture, 22 November 1916. From: W. G. Monckton. Scrapbooks. NZMS 1577.

  • Liberty loan

    New Zealand borrowed more than ₤80 million to cover war expenses - rations, clothing, personal equipment, horses, guns and equipment. Two thirds of this was borrowed from New Zealand citizens, mainly as low-interest war bonds.
    Flyer. Liberty loan, 15 August 1917. From: Lusty family. Collection of newspapers and ephemera. NZMS 1387.

  • Help! Read the words of our boys

    Thousands of women knitted socks and other comforts, baked cakes, gathered books, magazines and tobacco to send to the troops. These were packed and sent to distribution centres in England run by organisations such as the Red Cross.

    The item a soldier looked for most was mail from home. Mail delivery could be uncertain. It could be lost at sea, or fail to reach the addressee as soldiers were constantly on the move:

    "Some say that it was the inward mail that went to the bottom, which if true is sad news for us, since inward mails are so few and far between." – Bert Mitchell. 21 November 1915.
    Flyer. Help! Read the words of our boys at the front. 1918. From: Lusty family. Collection of newspapers and ephemera. NZMS 1387.
