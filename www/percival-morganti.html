• Paybook
    Percival Morganti was born and brought up in Reefton on the West Coast. He served on the Western Front. The tickets, flyers, programmes and cuttings kept by Percy held memories for him which we cannot know. But we can trace his war through them in combination with his military personnel record.

    All the items shown in this case come from the Morganti family papers (NZMS 1212)

    The photograph in Percy’s paybook is of his brother Charles Victor, who was reported Missing in September 1916. In April the following year he was declared Killed in Action by a Court of Enquiry.
    Paybook.
  • Leave Pass
    Percy enlisted on 15 November 1915 and embarked for overseas on 1 May 1916 on the Ulimaroa. His embarkation leave pass is dated 28 April but was extended to the hour of sailing.
    Leave pass, 1916.
  • Russell Soldiers Home
    En route to England, Percy spent nearly seven weeks in Egypt. For many soldiers, Egypt was their first experience of a country outside New Zealand and embodied the exoticism and temptations of the Orient. Clubs such as the Russell Soldiers Home, strategically located in central Cairo, offered more familiar home comforts of "newspapers, magazines, games, writing material", a refreshment bar and "bright Gospel services on Sundays".
    Russell Soldiers Home Cairo card, photograph of a Pyramid.

    From 28 November 1916 Percy was appointed Assistant Signal instructor based at Chelsea Barracks in London. The cloakroom ticket from Victoria Station and Underground ticket are dated for New Year’s Eve 1916.

  • Leave pass: Front
    Percy left for France on 17 May 1917 and was wounded and gassed in early August. After six weeks at Brockenhurst General Hospital he was transferred to the Convalescent Hospital at Hornchurch in Essex.

  • NZ Soldiers Medical Club flyer: Front
    The New Zealand Soldiers’ Medical Club close to the camp at Hornchurch was established by Ettie Rout as part of her ongoing campaign to reduce the spread of venereal disease among the troops. Prophylactic kits were available for purchase at the Club. Later in 1917 free kits were issued to soldiers going on leave and the VD rate dropped.
    NZ Soldiers Medical Club flyer.

  • Concert programme.
    From Hornchurch, Percy was sent to Codford Depot to improve his fitness before going back to the front. His health deteriorated and after another spell in Brockenhurst Hospital and then Codford again, he was classified unfit by a Medical Board. Percy embarked for New Zealand on the hospital ship Paparoa on 8 August 1918.

    He probably attended the illuminated concert in Lymington while at Brockenhurst.
    Concert programme, 1918.
  • Letter from Roy O. Dalrymple
    Roy Dalrymple had been classified unfit in 1917 and sent back to New Zealand. He wrote to “Morgi" describing the voyage home on the Maheno:

    “I have been in civies for a long time now & having a bung lung & trench fever they have given me my final ticket. Had a brute of a trip out in bed all the time. About 8 st 10 lbs when I got home for home treatment after a week in hospital. Just about normal again now."
    Roy O. Dalrymple. Letter, 20 October 1918.
  • P. Morganti in Rotorua
    Percy is on the far right in this photograph taken in Rotorua. It is undated but may have been taken during a further period of convalescence such as that mentioned in Roy Dalrymple’s letter. A convalescent hospital had been established at Rotorua in early 1916.
    Photograph. J. Cummings, Jack Lester, Ernie Fair and P. Morganti in Rotorua.
